Statistics

Statistics is a branch of mathematics that deals with collecting, analyzing, interpreting, and visualizing empirical data. Descriptive statistics and inferential statistics are the two major areas of statistics. Descriptive statistics are for describing the properties of sample and population data (what has happened). Inferential statistics use those properties to test hypotheses, reach conclusions, and make predictions (what can you expect).

Statistics is used in Machine Learning through asking questions about the data, preprocessing and cleaning the data, feature engineering of the data (selecting most relevent features), Model evaluation and prediction.

Types of Data

there are 2 types of data we get as Numerical and Categorical which we need to handle and analyze.

Numerical Data

Numerical data simply means floats or integers. Numeric data is further divided into 2 categories; discrete and continuous numerical variables.

  1. Discrete Numerical variables: Discrete variables are those whose values can only take on a finite or countably infinite set of values. For example, rank in the classroom, number of students in the class, number of cars in parking lot, etc. Discrete data can only take certain values. For example, you cant have 3.5 students or 7.2 cars.

  2. Continuous Numeric variable: Continuous variables are those whose value can range infinite, means not in the proper range for example salary of an employee, height of a person, time it takes to run a mile. For example, a persons height could be any value within the range of human heights, not just certain fixed heights.

Categorical Data

This type of data can be stored and identified based on their names or labels. It lacks a specific numerical value and is easily measured numerically. There are two types of categorical data: nominal and ordinal.

  1. Nominal Data: This type of data consists of two or more categories without any specific order. They cannot be quantified, that is, put into any definite hierarchy. Examples include hair color (blonde, brown, black, etc.), gender (male, female), and place of residence (New York, London, Sydney).

  2. Ordinal Data: This type of data consists of categories with a natural rank order. However, the difference between the ranks may not be equal. Examples include levels of education (high school, bachelors, masters), levels of work satisfaction (extremely satisfied, satisfied, neutral, unsatisfied, very unsatisfied), and star ratings (1-star, 2-star, 3-star, 4-star, 5-star).

Population and Sample

Population

In statistics, a population is the entire set of items or individuals from which data can be drawn. It represents the total scope of data your research is focusing on. For example, if youre studying the average height of adult humans, the population would be all adult humans.

Sample

A sample, on the other hand, is a subset of the population that is selected for the study. Its a smaller group chosen from the population, and it should be representative of the population. For example, if youre studying the average height of adult humans, a sample might be a group of 1,000 adult humans randomly selected from various locations. The reason we often work with samples instead of entire populations in statistics is that populations can be too large to measure completely. Collecting data from a sample is more practical and cost-effective.

Here are some key points to remember:

  • The size of the sample is always less than the total size of the population.
  • More than one sample can be derived from the same population.
  • The goal is to use the sample to make inferences about the population.

A measure of central tendency is a single value that represents the center point or the typical value of a dataset. It provides us with a single value that describes the center of a dataset. This helps us understand a dataset much more quickly compared to simply looking at all of the individual values in the dataset.

It is calculated by summing all the values and dividing by the number of values. when data contains outliers then finding mean and using it in any kind of manipulation is not suggested because a single outlier affects mean badly. so its solution is median.

Measures of Spread

Also called measures of dispersion are used to describe the variability or spread in a dataset. it helps answer the question: Where my data is more spread (positive, negative, center)

Range

In statistics, the range is a measure of the spread or dispersion of a dataset. It is calculated as the difference between the largest and smallest values in a set of data. These largest and smallest values are also known as the sample maximum and minimum. The formula to calculate the range is: R=HLR=HL where:

RR is the range
HH is the highest value in the data
LL is the lowest value in the data

The range provides an indication of statistical dispersion. It is a simple measure of variability and is expressed in the same units as the data. However, its worth noting that the range can be misleading when you have outliers in your data set. One extreme value in the data will give you a completely different range. To get a clear idea of your data variability, the range is best used in combination with other measures of variability like interquartile range and standard deviation.

Interquartile Range (IQR)

The IQR is a measure of statistical dispersion, which is the spread of the data. It is defined as the difference between the 75th75th and 25th25th percentiles of the data. The IQR measures the spread of the middle half of your data. It is the range for the middle 50% of your sample. Use the IQR to assess the variability where most of your values lie. Larger values indicate that the central portion of your data spread out further.

Probability

Probability is a mathematical framework for quantifying our uncertainty. It provides a formalism for reasoning about the likelihood of events. In the context of machine learning, we often use probability to make predictions.

Basic Concepts of Probability

Here are some basic concepts of probability that are important for machine learning:

  1. Experiment: An experiment is a procedure that yields one of a given set of possible outcomes.
  2. Sample Space: The sample space is the set of all possible outcomes of an experiment.
  3. Event: An event is a subset of the sample space.
  4. Probability: The probability of an event is a measure of the likelihood that the event will occur.

Probability Distributions

A probability distribution is a function that describes the likelihood of obtaining the possible values that a random variable can assume. In other words, the values of the variable vary based on the underlying probability distribution.

Types of Probability Distributions

Discrete Probability Distributions

These are used when the variable can take on a countable number of values. Examples include the Bernoulli, Binomial, and Poisson distributions.

Continuous Probability Distributions

These are used when the variable can take on an infinite number of values within a certain range. Examples include the Normal, Exponential, and Beta distributions.

The Empirical Rule, also known as the 68-95-99.7 rule, quantifies the proportion of data falling within certain intervals around the mean in a normal distribution. It provides a quick way to estimate the spread of data without performing detailed calculations.

Within one standard deviation of the mean (Mean 1 SD), approximately 68% of the data is expected to fall.
Within two standard deviations of the mean (Mean 2 SD), approximately 95% of the data is expected to fall.

Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

In simple terms, CLT tells us that the average of many samples (Sample mean) from the same population tends to form a normal distribution regardless of the shape of the population.

Imagine you have a large jar of differently colored candies. Each color represents a different flavor. Now, you close your eyes and pick a handful of candies. You note down the number of each flavor you picked. This is your first sample.

You put the candies back, mix them up, and repeat the process. Each time, you note down the number of each flavor. After doing this many times, you end up with a list of averages for each flavor from all your samples.

The Central Limit Theorem says that if you plot these averages on a graph, the shape you get will look like a bell curve, also known as a normal distribution, no matter what the original distribution of flavors was in the jar. This will be more accurate the more times you repeat the process.

Skewness

Skewness is a statistical measure that describes the asymmetry of a data distribution. It quantifies the degree to which the data deviates from a perfectly symmetrical distribution, such as a normal (bell-shaped) distribution.

Here are the types of skewness:

  1. Positive Skewness (Right-skewed): The right tail (larger values) is longer than the left tail (smaller values). The mean is greater than the median.
  2. Negative Skewness (Left-skewed): The left tail (smaller values) is longer than the right tail (larger values). The mean is less than the median.
  3. No Skewness: The distribution is symmetric. This does not necessarily mean the distribution is normal.

Hypothesis Testing

Hypothesis testing is a statistical method that is used to make decisions or inferences about population parameters based on sample data. In the context of machine learning, its often used to validate assumptions, compare models, or check the significance of features.

Here are the key steps involved in hypothesis testing:

  1. Formulate a Hypothesis: Begin with a null hypothesis (H0) and an alternative hypothesis (Ha). The null hypothesis represents the default assumption, while the alternative hypothesis challenges it.
  2. Choose a Significance Level (): Typically 0.05, this is the probability of rejecting the null hypothesis when its actually true.
  3. Calculate Test statistic and P-Value: Gather evidence (data) and calculate a test statistic.
  4. Make a Decision: If the p-value is less than or equal to , you reject the null hypothesis in favor of the alternative.

Some popular hypothesis tests are:

  • Chi-square test
  • T-test
  • Z-test
  • Analysis of Variance (ANOVA)

The Chi-square test, often denoted as , is a statistical test used to determine if there is a significant difference between the observed and expected frequencies in one or more categories. Its used for data that consist of variables distributed across various categories.

The formula for the Chi-square test is: 2=(OiEi)2Ei2=(OiEi)2Ei where: 22 represents the Chi-Square statistic
OiOi is the observed frequency (actual value)
EiEi is the expected frequency
The symbol represents the summation of values for all categories.
There are three main types of Chi-square tests:

Goodness of Fit Test: Tests whether the observed sample distribution matches an expected distribution.
Test of Independence: Tests whether two categorical variables are related to each other.
Test for Homogeneity: Tests whether the different categories of one variable are equally distributed across the categories of another variable.

Example:

Suppose a shop owner claims that an equal number of customers come into his shop each weekday. He records the number of customers that come into the shop on a given week and finds the following distribution: Monday: 50, Tuesday: 60, Wednesday: 40, Thursday: 47, Friday: 53. He can use a Chi-Square Goodness of Fit Test to determine if the distribution of the customers that come in each day is consistent with his hypothesized distribution.

# Observed data
observed_frequencies = [50, 60, 40, 47, 53]

# Total number of observations
total_observations = sum(observed_frequencies)
print(f"Total observations: {total_observations}")

# Expected frequency assuming equal distribution
expected_frequency = total_observations / len(observed_frequencies)
print(f"Expected frequency: {expected_frequency}")
expected_frequencies = [expected_frequency] * len(observed_frequencies)
print(f"Expected frequencies: {expected_frequencies}")

# Perform Chi-Square test
chi_square_statistic, p_value = chisquare(observed_frequencies, f_exp=expected_frequencies)

print(f"Chi-square statistic: {chi_square_statistic}")
print(f"P-value: {p_value}")

The Chi-Square test results in the given example can be interpreted as follows:

Chi-square statistic: The Chi-square statistic value was calculated to be 4.36. This value represents the sum of the squared difference between the observed and expected frequencies, divided by the expected frequency, for each category (in this case, each weekday).
P-value: The P-value was calculated to be 0.359. This value represents the probability of obtaining the observed frequencies by chance, given that the null hypothesis is true. The null hypothesis in this case is that the distribution of customers that come into the shop each weekday is consistent with the shop owners hypothesized distribution.

Since the P-value is greater than 0.05, we do not reject the null hypothesis. This means that the observed distribution of customers is not significantly different from the hypothesized distribution, assuming an equal number of customers come into the shop each weekday. In other words, the shop owners claim that an equal number of customers come into his shop each weekday cannot be statistically disproven based on this weeks data.

The t-test is used to compare the means of two groups to determine if they are significantly different from each other. The formula for the t-statistic in a two-sample t-test is: t=X1X2s21n1+s22n2t=X1X2s12n1+s22n2

Here:

X1X1 and X2X2 are the sample means of group 1 and group 2, respectively.
s21s12 and s22s22 are the sample variances of group 1 and group 2, respectively.
n1n1 and n2n2 are the sizes of group 1 and group 2, respectively.
The denominator of the formula is the standard error of the difference between two means. It measures the variability of the difference between these two sample means.

The t-statistic follows a t-distribution, which is similar to the normal distribution but has thicker tails. The degrees of freedom for the t-distribution is typically the total sample size minus 2.

Once the t-statistic is calculated, it is compared to a critical value from the t-distribution to determine whether the difference between the group means is statistically significant. The critical value depends on the chosen significance level (commonly 0.05) and the degrees of freedom.

If the absolute value of the t-statistic is greater than the critical value, we reject the null hypothesis and conclude that there is a significant difference between the group means. If the absolute value of the t-statistic is less than the critical value, we fail to reject the null hypothesis and conclude that there is not a significant difference between the group means.

Please note that while the t-test provides a formal framework for making inferences based on sample data, it does not replace the need for careful data exploration and understanding the problem domain.
